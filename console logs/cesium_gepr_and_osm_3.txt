       2 -
       3 - ## Overview
       4 - This document summarizes the architectural differences

       5 -
       6 - ## The Traversal Bomb: Spatial LOD Enforcement vs. Depth
         Limitations
       7 -
       8 - The primary discrepancy that allowed OSM assets to render
         beautifully while causing catastrophic stalling for GEPR
         assets lies in how spatial Level of Detail (LOD) was
         enforced.
       9 -
      10 - ### The OSM Optimization
      11 - To guarantee that the target architectural geometry
         (e.g., St. Paul's Cathedral) and its surrounding urban
         context loaded correctly, the pipeline utilized an
         unconditional spatial refinement rule:
      12 - ```cpp
      13 - if (distToOrigin < 2000.0f) shouldRefine = true;
      14 - if (depth < 4) shouldRefine = true;
      15 - ```
      16 - **Why it succeeds for OSM:**
      17 - OpenStreetMap asset quadtrees are extremely shallow
         (typically bottoming out at Depth 4 or 5), and the payloads
         consist of lightweight, untextured polygons. Forcing the
         engine to refine *everything* within a 2-kilometer radius
         simply causes it to rapidly hit the leaf nodes, pulling in
         the entire city blocks efficiently without overloading
         memory or bandwidth.
      18 -
      19 - ### The GEPR Bottleneck
      20 - **Why it destroys GEPR performance:**
      21 - GEPR tilesets are massive, heavily textured `.b3dm` files
         with deep hierarchical quadtrees extending to **Depth
         25+**.
      22 - Applying the unconditional `2000.0f` radius forced
         refinement completely overrides the dynamic Screen-Space
         Error (`targetSSE`) optimizations. As a result, the engine
         attempts to drill down to Depth 25 for *every single node*
         within the 2km radius.
      23 -
      24 - This triggers an explosive combinatorial fetch:
      25 - - **Bandwidth Flooding:** Tens of thousands of massive,
         high-resolution photogrammetry tiles are queued
         simultaneously.
      26 - - **Queue Saturation:** Even with our hyper-aggressive
         async queue of `128` simultaneous sockets, the bandwidth
         chokes, and the headless execution stalls waiting for the
         bottomless depth of GEPR to download.
      27 -
      28 - ## The Solution: Clamped Refinement
      29 - In the original separated `CesiumGEPR.h` pipeline, forced
         refinement was strictly clamped by both distance *and*
         depth to prevent this explosion:
      30 - ```cpp
      31 - if (distToOrigin < 50.0f && depth < 25) shouldRefine =

      32 - ```
      33 - This ensured that only the immediate target (a tiny

      41 - ## Implemented Solutions for GEPR (Iteration 2)
      42 - ### 1. Dynamic targetSSE & Depth Clamping
      43 - To balance the `targetSSE` heuristic against the massive
         scale of GEPR root nodes (which have an intrinsic SSE of
         ~12.89), we implemented a stepped ring of detail based on
         surface proximity (`distToBox`), falling back to a global
         target of 8.0 to ensure Earth root nodes refine:
      44 - - `< 2000.0f` -> `targetSSE = 4.0` (Low-poly city
         context)
      45 - - `< 800.0f` -> `targetSSE = 2.0` (Medium density urban
         blocks)
      46 - - `< 200.0f` -> `targetSSE = 1.0` (High architectural
         fidelity)
      47 - - `< 150.0f` -> `shouldRefine = true` **WITH** a strict
         `depth < 20` clamp (Targeting the St. Paul's Cathedral
         Dome).
      48 -
      49 - ### 2. Camera Frustum Precision (Artifact Fix)
      50 - When rendering large architectural meshes from an orbital
         distance (e.g., 1000m), an aggressive near clip plane
         (e.g., `0.1f`) wastes floating-point precision, causing
         depth buffer Z-fighting and severe fragmented culling of
         foreground meshes in the Reversed-Z pipeline.
      51 - **Fix:** The near clip plane in
         `makeInfiniteReversedZProjRH` has been pushed to `2.0f`.
         This ensures depth bits are concentrated exactly where the
         Cathedral geometry sits, resolving the fragmented "green
         screen" artifacts without affecting distant horizons.

      53 - ### 3. Build & Headless Execution Optimizations
      54 - Our fast "Ping-Pong" iteration loop relies on incredibly
         tight execution cycles. The build path has been audited and
         structurally hardened to minimize overhead:
      55 - - **LLD Linker:** `lld-link.exe` is utilized, which
         dramatically cuts down linking time for the large
         pre-compiled `vcpkg` static libraries (Curl, GLM,
         OpenNurbs).
      56 - - **Ninja Build System:** Rapid parallel C++ object
         compilation.
      57 - - **Implicit Release Optimization:** `build_and_run.bat`
         specifically omits `-DCMAKE_BUILD_TYPE=Debug`. This
         triggers the `else()` branch in `CMakeLists.txt`,
         automatically building `AliceViewer.exe` with `/MT /O2
         /Ob2`. This guarantees the headless execution utilizes
         hyper-optimized machine code for the massive tree
         traversals, saving seconds off the async parsing loop.

      59 - ## Identified Missing Tricks & Upcoming Exploration
         Trajectories
      60 - A rigorous review of historical successful architectures
         (e.g. `anti_gravity_cesium_gepr_success.md` and the
         `avon-ces-int-1` parallel branch) has revealed several
         critical mechanisms that did NOT survive the unified
         integration pipeline in `cesium_gepr_and_osm.h`. These form
         the core of our next exploration trajectories:
      61 -
      62 - ### Trajectory 1: Y-Up to Z-Up Coordinate Fix
      63 - - **The Issue:** `glTF` payloads inside `.b3dm` files are
         natively Y-Up, but Cesium's ECEF `RTC_CENTER` is strictly
         Z-Up. Applying local coordinates directly to the ECEF
         offset without rotation causes extreme spatial tearing
         ("giant triangles").
      64 - - **The Fix:** We must inject a strict rotation matrix
         (`x -> x, y -> -z, z -> y`) into the `cgltf` parser
         *before* applying the `RTC_CENTER` translation.
      65 -
      66 - ### Trajectory 2: Aggressive Intermediate LOD Skipping
      67 - - **The Issue:** Unconditionally fetching multi-megabyte
         payloads for Earth-scale nodes when our target is a
         specific Cathedral causes unnecessary bandwidth flooding.
      68 - - **The Fix:** Implement a strict logic bypass: if
         `shouldRefine == true` and a node has children, we must
         skip fetching its payload entirely and proceed straight to
         the highest available detail.
      69 -
      70 - ### Trajectory 3: Distance Metric (`distToOrigin` vs.
         `distToBox`)
      71 - - **The Issue:** Using surface proximity (`distToBox`)
         can cause massive Earth-scale root nodes to constantly
         evaluate to a distance of `0.0`, triggering unintended
         forced refinement.
      72 - - **The Fix:** Evaluate reverting to `distToOrigin` (box
         center) to naturally filter out massive parent nodes whose
         centers are located deep underground or across the ocean.
      73 -
      74 - ### Trajectory 4: The Hard Horizon Cull (5KM Limit)
      75 - - **The Issue:** Even with dynamic SSE, distant
         high-contrast geometry on the horizon can still trigger
         refinement, eating bandwidth.
      76 - - **The Fix:** Implement a brute-force distance cull
         (e.g., `distToBox2 > 25000000.0f` for nodes deeper than
         depth 8) to explicitly prevent high-resolution textures
         from loading beyond 5 kilometers.
